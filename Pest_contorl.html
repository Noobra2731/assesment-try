Pest Control Strategies

Learn methods to protect crops from pests and diseases effectively.

Aphids (Sap-sucking insects)

Aphids are small, soft-bodied insects that appear green, black, or yellow. They feed on plant sap by piercing stems and leaves, causing leaf curling, yellowing, and stunted growth. They also transmit viral diseases between plants.

  • Biological: Release natural predators like ladybird beetles and lacewings that feed on aphids.
  • Organic: Apply neem oil, garlic extract, or a mild soap-water spray on affected areas.
  • Chemical: Use insecticides like imidacloprid or malathion according to recommended doses.

Cotton Bollworm (Helicoverpa armigera)

A serious pest affecting cotton, pulses, and tomatoes. The larvae bore into buds, flowers, and fruits, causing severe yield loss and poor quality produce.

  • Biological: Apply Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) or release Trichogramma parasitoids.
  • Organic: Use pheromone traps to detect and monitor adult moths.
  • Chemical: Spray insecticides like cypermethrin or chlorpyrifos during early infestation.

Red Spider Mite (Tetranychus spp.)

Tiny red or brown mites that attack leaves, causing yellow spots and leaf drop. They thrive in dry, hot weather and damage crops like cotton, brinjal, and beans.

  • Biological: Encourage predatory mites such as Phytoseiulus persimilis.
  • Organic: Use sulfur dust, neem extract, or soap sprays.
  • Chemical: Apply acaricides like dicofol or abamectin if infestation is heavy.

Fruit Fly (Bactrocera spp.)

A common pest of fruits like mango, guava, papaya, and cucurbits. Female flies lay eggs inside the fruit, and larvae feed on the pulp, leading to rotting and premature fruit fall.

  • Biological: Introduce parasitoid wasps like Diachasmimorpha longicaudata.
  • Organic: Install traps with protein bait or molasses solution.
  • Chemical: Use spinosad or malathion baits around fruit trees.

Purpose of the Section

The Pest Control Section educates farmers about identifying, preventing, and managing pests responsibly. It promotes Integrated Pest Management (IPM) — combining biological, organic, and chemical methods to minimize crop loss while protecting the environment.
